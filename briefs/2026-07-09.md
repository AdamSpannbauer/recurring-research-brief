## Section 1: Top 5 Papers

1. **Beyond IID: How General Are Tabular Foundation Models, Really?**  
   **Authors:** Lennart Purucker, Andrej Tschalzev, Nick Erickson, Gioia Blayer, David Holzmüller, Alan Arazi, Alexander Pfefferle, Mustafa Tajjar, Gaël Varoquaux, Frank Hutter  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv  
   **Release date:** June 29, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30410))  
   This is the most useful tabular-FM paper this week because it directly attacks benchmark myopia. The authors introduce **BeyondArena**, a 142-dataset benchmark spanning IID, temporal, and grouped settings; dataset sizes from small to million-row scale; high-dimensional tables; text columns; and high-cardinality features. The central finding is sobering: current tabular foundation models look strong on tiny-to-medium IID settings, but tree-based and conventional deep models still dominate many non-IID, large, and high-dimensional regimes. The accompanying **Data Foundry** schema/framework may matter as much as the benchmark.  
   **Why you should care:** This is likely to become a reference point for deciding whether new tabular FMs are actually foundational or just overfit to comfortable benchmark slices.

2. **CausalMix: Data Mixture as Causal Inference for Language Model Training**  
   **Authors:** Zinan Tang, Yukun Zhang, Shaomian Zheng, Zhuoshi Pan, Qizhi Pei, Dingnan Jin, Jun Zhou, Yujun Wang, Biqing Huang  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv  
   **Release date:** July 1, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01104))  
   CausalMix reframes data-mixture optimization for LLM training as a causal-treatment problem: pool statistics are covariates, domain mixture is treatment, and downstream performance is the response. The authors fit a causal model over 512 Qwen2.5-0.5B runs to estimate CATEs, then extrapolate mixture choices to an 800K-token pool and a 7B model; they also test long chain-of-thought data on Qwen3-4B-Base. The idea is not just better data selection, but a portable, interpretable model of how mixtures behave under distribution shift.  
   **Why you should care:** It is a concrete bridge between causal ML and foundation-model data curation, with an appealing recipe for avoiding endless proxy retraining.

3. **WARP: Weight-Space Analysis for Recovering Training Data Portfolios**  
   **Authors:** Tzu-Heng Huang, Aditya Goyal, John Cooper, Frederic Sala  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv; ICML 2026 Workshop on Weight-Space Symmetries  
   **Release date:** July 2, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01686))  
   WARP asks whether released weights contain a recoverable signature of the training-data mixture. Instead of inferring individual members, it estimates global domain proportions from a fine-tuned model. The trick is to interpolate between base and fine-tuned weights using model merging, creating pseudo-checkpoints that approximate the missing training trajectory. Geometric features extracted from these footprints are mapped to mixture weights with either a softmax readout or MLP trained on synthetic mixtures. Reported MAE is low in controlled BERT/GPT-2 settings, and the framing is unusually relevant to data provenance.  
   **Why you should care:** If it scales, data-mixture auditing may become a weight-space problem rather than a black-box membership-inference problem.

4. **Privacy Vulnerabilities of Attention Layers in Tabular Foundation Models and Protection of High-Risk Queries**  
   **Authors:** Tânia Carvalho, Maxime Cordy  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv  
   **Release date:** June 24, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26021))  
   This paper challenges the comforting assumption that tabular FMs are privacy-benign because they are often pretrained on synthetic data. The real vulnerability is in-context learning: sensitive labeled context rows are supplied at inference time. The proposed **AMIA** attack uses attention concentration to perform shadow-model-free membership inference and beats confidence-based baselines, especially in low-FPR regimes. The defense is inference-time and k-anonymity-inspired: identify high-risk queries and reduce uniqueness in context-key representations, cutting AMIA leakage substantially with modest utility loss.  
   **Why you should care:** Tabular FMs are moving toward deployment exactly where in-context rows are sensitive; this gives a specific attack surface and mitigation target.

5. **How Data Shapes RoPE Frequency Usage: From Positional Scale Matching to Length Generalization**  
   **Authors:** Xinyi Wu, Siyuan Liu, Ali Jadbabaie  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv  
   **Release date:** July 8, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07678))  
   This is a clean theory/empirics paper on why trained transformers use RoPE frequencies non-uniformly. The authors propose that training data selects positional frequencies to match the distribution of relative-distance dependencies. RoPE frequencies are treated as “positional lenses” with a field-resolution tradeoff; for a dependency profile of width \(W\), the preferred frequency scales like \(1/W\). The paper also explains when position interpolation helps length generalization: it works when test-time dependencies are approximate dilations of training-time dependencies, which natural language appears to satisfy partially.  
   **Why you should care:** It turns long-context generalization from a tuning heuristic into a data-geometry question.

## Section 2: Venue Watch

- **ICML 2026 Workshop on Foundation Models for Structured Data — accepted papers.** The FMSD list is the densest current snapshot of structured-data FM work. Spotlight orals include dataset inference for provenance/privacy auditing in tabular FMs, zero-shot blind spots in time-series FMs, HEPA for self-supervised horizon-conditioned event prediction, and SurvivalPFN for in-context Bayesian survival prediction. The poster list shows a broad shift from “tabular prediction” toward **structured foundation models as infrastructure**: causal foundation models, synthetic time-series DAG priors, discrete-choice validity audits, relational context-window failures, clinical zero-shot prediction, supply-chain risk, and LLM-guided constraints for synthetic tabular data. ([icml-structured-fm-workshop.github.io](https://icml-structured-fm-workshop.github.io/accepted-papers/))

- **ICML 2026 Workshop on Weight-Space Symmetries.** This workshop sits at the intersection of theory, merging, compression, uncertainty, and model auditing. Its official page frames weight space as structured by permutation, rotation, scaling, head/expert symmetries, and emphasizes applications to training dynamics, loss landscapes, model merging, and learning from weights. The accepted-paper list includes WARP, Hodge-style merge failure diagnosis, quotient Fréchet averages, LoRA gauge symmetry, symmetry-induced non-identifiability in neural circuit inference, and Bayesian posterior geometry. ([weightsymmetry.com](https://www.weightsymmetry.com/))

- **JMLR Volume 27 latest stream.** New visible additions include a fine-grained risk bound connecting domain invariance and domain diversity; theory for autoregressive video diffusion error accumulation and history forgetting; metric-space-valued deep regression via weighted Fréchet means; mixing-time bounds for high-dimensional probit Gibbs samplers; abstract gradient training for poisoning/unlearning/DP certification; and approximation-free differentiable oblique decision trees. The current stream is unusually strong on theory that clarifies evaluation failures and representation/generalization mechanisms. ([jmlr.org](https://www.jmlr.org/))

- **M&SOM Volume 28 Issue 3 and MSOM 2026.** The May–June issue has a broad operations mix: an OM Forum vision statement on AI-era supply chains, feature-based choice models, behavioral assortment planning, order-ahead queueing, nearshoring under regional value-content rules, data-driven prescriptive analytics with side information, spatial demand learning for mobile facility location, and disaster-relief stochastic integer programming. The MSOM annual conference is imminent, July 12–14, 2026 at Harvard Business School, with SIG Day on July 12. ([pubsonline.informs.org](https://pubsonline.informs.org/toc/msom/current))

## Section 3: Emerging Trends

- **Tabular FMs are entering their “adversarial benchmark” phase.** BeyondArena, FMSD privacy/provenance papers, AMIA, and the privacy competition all point away from headline accuracy and toward non-IID generalization, context leakage, dataset provenance, and economic validity.

- **Causality is being used as engineering glue, not just inference theory.** CausalMix uses CATE-style thinking for data mixtures; FMSD has causal FMs for time series, continuous treatments, partial identification, and tabular few-shot learning with causal information.

- **Weight space is becoming an observable data structure.** WARP and the WSS workshop suggest a growing belief that training data, optimization, symmetries, and mergeability leave recoverable geometric traces in weights.

- **Representation theory is becoming more data-conditioned.** The RoPE paper and JMLR domain-generalization work both argue that learned representations cannot be understood only architecturally; dataset dependency profiles and domain decompositions matter.

- **Structured-data research is converging with privacy and systems.** The most interesting tabular work now involves context management, leakage, provenance, data curation schemas, and deployment constraints, not just model architecture.

## Section 4: Worth Watching

- **BeyondArena / Data Foundry / TabArena-v0.2 path.** The authors explicitly position BeyondArena as a step toward TabArena-v0.2 and release links for benchmark code, Data Foundry, curation notes, and a Hugging Face dataset. This should become a recurring evaluation target for any serious tabular-FM work. ([huggingface.co](https://huggingface.co/papers/2606.30410))

- **Privacy Attacks on Tabular Foundation Models competition.** Submitted to the NeurIPS 2026 Competition Track, this benchmark has four tracks: attribute inference, membership inference, dataset inference, and property inference against tabular FMs in the in-context regime. The starting kit was released June 29 and frozen July 6; registration and leaderboards are scheduled to open July 20. ([sprintml.com](https://sprintml.com/blog/2026-05-16-privacy-tabular-fm/))

- **AgenticInterpBench / HyVE.** “Can Language Model Agents be Helpful Circuit Explainers in Mechanistic Interpretability?” introduces 84 semi-synthetic transformer circuits with 163 component annotations and an agent loop for hypothesize–validate–explain. The finding that validation failures dominate is useful for anyone building automated interpretability workflows. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24026))

- **Symbolic Mechanistic Data Attribution.** SMDA combines SAE features with closed-form ridge attribution to explain how supervised fine-tuning examples shift symbolic behavioral policies. It is early and narrow, but the “training data → feature shifts → policy coefficients” pipeline is exactly the kind of hybrid interpretability/data-audit artifact worth tracking. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29171))
