## Section 1: Top 5 Papers

1. **GRAFT: Graph-Matched Retrieval and Fusion of Tables in Data Lakes** — Daomin Ji, Hui Luo, Zhifeng Bao, Shane Culpepper, Shazia Sadiq. **arXiv cs.DB**, submitted **July 14, 2026**. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12717))  
   GRAFT is a strong data-management paper for the “agent over data lake” era. Rather than retrieve tables independently, it models retrieval as graph matching between a query-derived intent graph and a heterogeneous lake graph, coupling semantic relevance, join/union compatibility, and evidence diversity via an IGMS log-determinant reward. The subgraph generation problem is cast as an MDP, trained with implicit Q-learning over self-generated trajectories, then accelerated by online pruning. On Spider/BIRD-style lake tasks, it improves F1 and “sufficiency,” a more agent-relevant criterion than top-k table recall.  
   **Why you should care:** It reframes table retrieval as structured evidence assembly, which is exactly the missing layer between data discovery, entity integration, and agentic analytics.

2. **Extractable Memorization From First Principles** — A. Feder Cooper, Marika Swanberg, Jamie Hayes, Lea Duesterwald, Christopher De Sa, Daniel E. Ho, Mark A. Lemley, Percy Liang. **arXiv cs.LG/cs.CL**, submitted **July 14, 2026**. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12649))  
   This paper clarifies a messy concept: when does generating a training sequence actually constitute memorization? The authors argue that extraction claims require matched comparisons against comparable non-training sequences, separating memorization from ordinary predictability. They formalize two calibration procedures: a conformal test for population-level claims and a document-level census for single artifacts such as books. The empirical punchline is that many short-sequence extraction setups overstate memorization, while some true memorization is provable only through probability thresholds far below feasible sampling budgets.  
   **Why you should care:** It gives a statistically principled vocabulary for privacy, copyright, and memorization audits—useful well beyond LLMs.

3. **What Makes a Representational Prior Work? Feature Families, Label-Free Invariances, and Critical Windows in Grokking** — Gunner Levi Howe. **arXiv cs.LG**, submitted **July 14, 2026**. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12735))  
   This is a compact but conceptually sharp study of why some representation priors accelerate grokking and others fail. Across 188 runs, the paper argues that priors operate at the level of circuit-relevant feature families: a coherent but wrong prior can block generalization, while a label-free invariance prior over commuted pairs accelerates generalization reliably. The most interesting claim is temporal: the useful prior need only be applied early, suggesting a “critical window” for injecting structure before memorizing dynamics dominate.  
   **Why you should care:** It turns grokking from a curiosity into a controlled testbed for representation priors and self-supervised invariance design.

4. **Reducing information dependency does not cause training data privacy. Adversarially non-robust features do** — Rasmus Torp, Shailen K. Smith, Adam Breuer. **arXiv cs.LG / ICLR 2026**, submitted **July 14, 2026**. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12354))  
   This paper challenges a common story: that reducing information dependence or rote memorization is what protects models from reconstruction attacks. The authors report that models can remain vulnerable despite weak information-theoretic dependence, and can be robust to inversion despite maximal memorization. Their causal explanation ties reconstruction privacy to adversarially non-robust features—generalizable but imperceptible features that make inversion harder. They introduce Anti Adversarial Training to deliberately shift models toward these features, improving reconstruction defense and accuracy relative to prior defenses.  
   **Why you should care:** It suggests privacy leakage is not just “memorization plus capacity,” but is mediated by the learned representation’s robust/non-robust feature geometry.

5. **Forgetful Attention: A Trainable Support-Vector Memory with Certified Selection and Exact Unlearning** — Vishwajith Ramesh. **arXiv cs.LG**, submitted **July 13, 2026**. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12204))  
   Forgetful Attention replaces softmax-style context memory with a max-margin support-vector memory. Reserve tokens receive exactly zero weight, enabling certified eviction, and an incremental solver can delete a token to recover the same state as retraining without it under a fixed box parameter. The paper is honest about current costs: the exact certificate is not carried by the approximate training procedure, and throughput is far below a softmax baseline. But the experiments show meaningful rare-item recall, patient-record deletion, and better enwik8 compression than a sliding-window Transformer.  
   **Why you should care:** It is an intriguing route toward attention mechanisms that make deletion, eviction, and provenance first-class operations rather than afterthoughts.

## Section 3: Emerging Trends

- **Structured retrieval is becoming graph retrieval.** GRAFT and related lakehouse work suggest table retrieval is moving away from embedding-only matching toward graph-constrained evidence assembly: joinability, unionability, provenance, and downstream sufficiency are now part of the retrieval objective.

- **Privacy arguments are shifting from scalar dependence to representation geometry.** The memorization and reconstruction papers both push against simplistic narratives: extraction needs matched baselines, and inversion risk may depend more on robust/non-robust feature structure than on memorization alone.

- **Tabular foundation models are expanding sideways.** After TabFM/TabPFN-style tabular prediction, the next wave is applying tabular priors to time series, neural posterior estimation, structured health records, and data-lake reasoning—not merely improving OpenML scores.

- **Evaluation is becoming “full-denominator.”** SQL-RewriteBench, memorization calibration, and recent TMLR uncertainty papers all emphasize that systems should be judged over the entire path from input acceptance to correctness and usefulness, not just conditional performance after filtering failures.

- **Mechanistic interpretability is maturing into causal auditing.** SAE work this week again argues that geometric recovery is insufficient; features need ablation, steering, and activation-behavior validation before they should be trusted as meaningful units.

## Section 4: Worth Watching

- **SQL-RewriteBench** — a correctness-gated benchmark for statement-level SQL rewriting with 180 executable instances, checker contracts, and “full-denominator” accounting across acceptance, generation, execution, result consistency, unsafe rewrites, and speedup. The finding that all seven tested methods have negative full-benchmark CGOQ is a useful reality check for LLM-assisted database optimization. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09251))

- **Spider 2.0-AIFunc** — a 465-instance benchmark across 125 real-world databases for AI-native SQL functions on Snowflake, covering classification, filtering, sentiment analysis, extraction, similarity search, and aggregation inside SQL. It looks like an important bridge between text-to-SQL and production analytical workflows where LLM functions are database primitives. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06229))

- **Identifiability of Relational Queries in Multi-View Pretraining** — a theory/artifact paper showing that some downstream relational queries are structurally non-identifiable from exposed interface attributes, with a 1/2 minimax error floor and a polynomial certificate. This is highly relevant to data integration, multi-view pretraining, and claims that more records or bigger models can solve schema-interface ambiguity. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04735))

- **From Tables to Time: Extending TabPFN-v2 to Time Series Forecasting** — a TMLR July 2026 item by Shi Bin Hoo, Samuel Müller, David Salinas, and Frank Hutter. Worth tracking because it signals a direct attempt to export tabular foundation-model machinery into structured temporal prediction rather than building a time-series FM from scratch. ([jmlr.org](https://jmlr.org/tmlr/papers/))

- **MEDIC: Comprehensive Evaluation of Leading Indicators for LLM Safety and Utility in Clinical Applications** — a TMLR July 2026 benchmark/resource with code listed on Hugging Face. Even if clinical LLMs are adjacent, its “leading indicators” framing may influence evaluation design for high-stakes structured-data models. ([jmlr.org](https://jmlr.org/tmlr/papers/))
