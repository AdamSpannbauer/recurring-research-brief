## Section 1: Top 5 Papers

1. **Verbalizable Representations Form a Global Workspace in Language Models**  
   **Authors:** Wes Gurnee, Nicholas Sofroniew, Adam Pearce, Mateusz Piotrowski, Isaac Kauvar, Runjin Chen, Anna Soligo, Paul Bogdan, Euan Ong, Rowan Wang, Ben Thompson, David Abrahams, Subhash Kantamneni, Emmanuel Ameisen, Joshua Batson, Jack Lindsey  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv cs.CL/cs.LG  
   **Date:** July 16, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15495))  
   This is the week’s highest-upside interpretability paper. It introduces the **Jacobian lens** to identify representations a model is “poised to verbalize,” arguing that these form a compact intermediate-layer **J-space** with global-workspace-like properties: reportability, deliberate recall, holding intermediate silent reasoning content, and broad downstream broadcast. The most provocative part is alignment-facing: the authors claim J-space exposes strategic deliberation, evaluation awareness, and trained-in dispositions absent from outputs, and propose counterfactual reflection training. Some claims are ambitious, but the technique could become a focal object for mechanistic audits.  
   **Why you should care:** If robust, this gives interpretability a concrete target between sparse features and behavior: the model’s internally reportable workspace.

2. **Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training**  
   **Authors:** Jingyan Shen, Ang Li, Salman Rahman, Yifan Sun, Micah Goldblum, Matus Telgarsky, Pavel Izmailov  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv cs.LG/cs.AI/cs.CL  
   **Date:** July 17, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097))  
   This paper attacks a key blind spot in reasoning-RL: pretraining and RL are usually analyzed separately. The authors build a controlled chess-language-model pipeline, pretraining 5M–1B parameter models on human chess games, SFT’ing on synthetic reasoning traces, and applying RL on verifiable chess puzzles. They find post-RL performance and RL learning-curve slopes are strongly predicted by pretraining loss/tokens, and that RL behaves differently by difficulty: amplifying SFT-favored moves on easy problems but surfacing nearly absent correct moves on hard ones. A math-domain 1B experiment shows the same pattern.  
   **Why you should care:** It offers a tractable experimental science of the pretraining–post-training interface, not just another RL recipe.

3. **Publicly-Verifiable Certificates for Statistical Algorithms**  
   **Authors:** Michael Ngo, Michael P. Kim  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv cs.LG/cs.CR/cs.DS  
   **Date:** July 17, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15528))  
   This theory paper proposes **publicly-verifiable certificates of statistical validity**: a learner publishes a hypothesis and certificate, and any user with their own distribution can efficiently check whether the result is valid for that distribution. The first construction focuses on adaptive statistical-query algorithms. For algorithms making \(k\) adaptive queries, their certificate sample complexity scales as \(O(\log k)\), contrasting with \(\tilde O(\sqrt{k})\)-type learning sample complexity. The idea reframes learning outputs as auditable artifacts, connecting adaptive data analysis, cryptographic proof systems, and distributionally robust validation.  
   **Why you should care:** This is a clean step toward verifiable ML claims: not “trust my training run,” but “check this certificate on your distribution.”

4. **Do Generative Models Keep Time? A Time-Aware Evaluation of Synthetic Sequential Tabular Data**  
   **Authors:** Kiwan Kwon, Kangmin Kim, Hojin Lee, Yeseong Jung, Hyeongwoo Kong, Vamsi K. Potluru, Saerom Park, Yongjae Lee  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv cs.LG/stat.ML  
   **Date:** July 17, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15606))  
   This is a timely evaluation paper for synthetic tabular data. The core point is simple and important: a sequential tabular generator can match marginals and relationships while producing temporally nonsensical records—backward timestamps, repeated times, or impossible entity trajectories. The authors define a taxonomy-driven protocol based on time representation, regularity, trajectory dependence, and schema/history links, then evaluate timestamp validity, aligned-time cross-sections, within-entity dynamics, time-varying relational structure, trajectory-level utility, and privacy. Across eight generators and thirteen datasets, conventional rankings disagree substantially with temporal evaluation.  
   **Why you should care:** Sequential synthetic data needs temporal validity tests, not static-table fidelity scores with timestamps attached.

5. **Diffusion Models Recover Accurate Mixture Weights Despite Score Function Insensitivity**  
   **Authors:** Andrew Dennehy, Ramchandran Muthukumar, Rebecca Willett, Nisha Chandramoorthy  
   **Venue/source:** arXiv cs.LG/stat.ML/math.PR  
   **Date:** July 16, 2026  
   **Link:** ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15485))  
   This paper resolves a subtle puzzle in diffusion modeling: target scores can be insensitive to mixture weights, yet trained diffusion models often recover relative mode amplitudes. The authors connect diffusion score-matching loss to parameter recovery from generated samples via a **diffusion score sensitivity index** measuring how loss varies with a target parameter across noise levels. For Gaussian mixtures in arbitrary dimension, they prove mixture-weight estimation error is on the same order as DSM loss under mild conditions. Empirically, intermediate noising can create sensitivity, while poor schedules can reduce it and amplify modes.  
   **Why you should care:** It gives a principled diagnostic for when diffusion objectives preserve distributional quantities beyond visual/sample quality.

## Section 3: Emerging Trends

- **Evaluation is moving from static benchmarks to structural validity.** Synthetic tabular work is now probing temporal and relational consistency; database benchmarks increasingly gate on executable correctness; agent workshops are emphasizing live interaction, latency, safety boundaries, and structured-data grounding.

- **Reasoning research is becoming pipeline-aware.** The pretraining-to-RL paper is a good example: the object of study is no longer “does RL improve reasoning?” but how pretraining loss, token budget, SFT traces, and verifiable rewards jointly determine post-training returns.

- **Interpretability is converging on intermediate “workspaces,” not only sparse features.** The J-space/global-workspace framing, activation-patching critiques from prior briefs, and recent representation-geometry papers all suggest a shift toward causal, task-relevant internal state variables.

- **Verification and certification are spreading into ML systems and statistics.** Publicly verifiable statistical certificates, abstract gradient training, database isolation verification, and correctness-gated SQL/semantic operators all point toward auditable ML/data artifacts.

- **Agentic systems are pulling databases, structured data, and systems research back into core ML.** NeurIPS workshop activity around structured-data agents, on-device agents, agent safety, and closed-loop systems suggests that “foundation model + data management + evaluation” will be a major 2026–2027 interface.

## Section 4: Worth Watching

- **Information-Directed Sampling for Causal Bandits** — Muhammad Qasim Elahi, Murat Kocaoglu, Mahsa Ghasemi. Bayesian causal bandits with non-manipulable context/post-intervention variables, causal Thompson sampling, and IDS regret bounds with Monte Carlo approximation error. Useful for intervention policies where causal structure shares information across arms. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15577))

- **Cluster-Aware Matching via Laplacian Optimal Transport** — Gabriel Samberg, YoonHaeng Hur, Yuehaw Khoo, Nir Sharon. A graph-Laplacian-regularized OT method for region-to-region matching and simultaneous clustering across point clouds; potentially relevant for representation alignment, record linkage with latent cluster structure, and multi-view matching. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16178))

- **From Interpretation to Compilation: Compilation-Based Execution of Semantic Operators** — Wenkai Dong, Yifan Wang. Vision paper, but the idea is crisp: treat LLMs as semantic compilers that generate physical operators, not as per-row interpreters. This is likely to recur in LLM/database systems. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13407))

- **Efficient and Effective In-place Graph-based Vector Index Updates** — Haotian Liu, Yujun He, Bo Tang. Yi targets dynamic vector indexes under frequent insert/delete workloads, reporting 1.75× update throughput and 1.8× concurrent-search throughput over prior systems on an 800M dataset. Worth tracking for retrieval systems and agent memory. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15576))

- **AgentSD: Agentic Systems for Structured Data** — NeurIPS 2026 workshop. Specific enough to suppress in future: it is positioning structured-data foundation models, databases, scientific ML, neuro-symbolic reasoning, schema exploration, and data-agent evaluation as one community. ([agent-sd.com](https://agent-sd.com/))
